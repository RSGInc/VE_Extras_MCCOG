Introduction

Overview

The Madison County Council of Governments (MCCOG) Metropolitan Transportation Plan (MTP) established a set of normative scenarios designed to compare future outcomes for the Anderson Metropolitan Planning Area (MPA). As part of this scenario planning effort, RSG has developed a VisionEval model for the region to assist in understanding the impacts of land use and policy changes in the MTP scenarios on travel behavior, greenhouse gas (GHG) reduction, and socioeconomic impact.

VisionEval has been designed as an alternative to traditional network-based travel models. The model is designed to produce per capita measures of vehicle miles traveled (VMT) and GHG but is also sensitive to changes in demographics, active travel, teleworking, and vehicle fleet options that are included in the MTP.

The report includes the following sections documenting the analysis of VMT and GHG reduction using VisionEval:

  • Chapter 1 is a summary of the VisionEval tool.

  • Chapter 2 a summary of the VisionEval reference model including inputs, model design, and reference model results.

  • Chapter 3 summarizes the scenario analysis.

VisionEval Introduction

{width="2.4368055555555554in" height="2.283333333333333in"}VisionEval modeling supports the development of the MCCOG modeling of household travel behavior. This document sets out the scenarios that are designed in VisionEval as well as the inputs within each of those scenarios.

VisionEval is a widely used tool for evaluating transportation policies and projects and has been employed in a variety of settings across the United States. It offers a flexible framework that can be used to analyze a range of policy options and transportation scenarios. VisionEval has been used by several state and regional planning agencies. It is most often, such as in the case of MCCOG, used early in the long-range planning process.

The model is used to assess a wide range of policy and pricing effects on household travel modes and preferences, including travel by automobile, transit, bicycle, and walking. In addition, VisionEval includes built-in metrics and analytical methods to summarize travel behavior changes as a result of land use changes and other influences such as policy, pricing, and vehicle fleet changes. VisionEval does not assess network impacts or precisely localized land use impacts and instead creates a broader picture of geographies within the model region. This makes VisionEval ideal for strategic modeling practices that assess specific performance metrics such as VMT or GHG.

Transportation planning models can be categorized into three levels, as shown in Figure 2: strategic, tactical, and operational. VisionEval is strategic model - an econometric approach to travel modeling using nationally estimated data on household-based characteristics combined with local data including attributes of the built form, the supply of available transportation modes and infrastructure, and pricing and policies that may influence travel behavior. Known as an aggregated supply and disaggregate demand model, it uses zonal level data to represent the amount and availability of travel infrastructure (i.e., supply) and a detailed synthetic representation of daily travel demand at the household level. By not assigning daily travel (or individual trips) to specific links on the network, the model runs faster and produces household estimates of travel demand that can be aggregated to zonal estimates of behavior. This structure makes the model ideal for "top of the funnel" exploration thanks to its flexibility, speed, and design to explore uncertainty and assess possible impacts of "what if" questions.

Traditional travel demand models that assign trips to a network can be referred to as tactical models. These models are better suited for assessing more engineering-level questions such as how many users may be on a particular link, or how will congestion or operations change as a result of changing other inputs. For instance, most trip-based network models are less sensitive to changes in pricing or factors that may change individual households trip generation potential.

Operational models are often used for detailed and more short-term operational questions. These models require a specific forecast for a subarea and are insensitive or ignorant to larger changes in demand that may occur with population changes or the price of fuel.

VisionEval estimates demand by using a nationally estimated set of data at the census block group level from the National Household Travel Survey (NHTS). Each of the sub-models in the VisionEval design require inputs that inform key characteristics of the household and its relationship to the area around it. VisionEval uses a sequence of modules that combine input data to build a composite representation of these characteristics for each household in the model region and estimate travel demand. This process follows four main steps:

1) Define the number and location of households, including key attributes such as the number of persons, their ages, employment status, occupation type, and income.

2) Determine the attributes of the area surrounding each household, including walkability, transit frequency, miles of arterials or freeways, the share of active travel in each zone, household and employment density, ratio of jobs to households, the ratio of single-family to multi-family units, and many others.

3) Assign travel modes and vehicle ownership to each household. Household characteristics and built form inform whether households are more likely to share vehicles or have one per driver, as well as vehicle age, types, and fuel type.

4) Define the pricing and policies that influence travel demand in the region. Establish key inputs that affect demand for travel such as prices of fuel, parking costs, travel demand management incentives, congestion pricing, and other policies and pricing inputs that may exist in the region.

The result is an estimate of daily travel demand for each household in the model region for each of the model years that inputs are provided.

VisionEval also contains a Multiscenario scenario framework that allows users to run hundreds of scenarios with variations that represent possible futures. This setup is ideal for testing travel outcomes under uncertainty by providing insight into unique or potentially unexpected "what if" scenarios for the model region. RSG has developed dashboard tools using Shiny for efficiently running a Multiscenario framework, and for both analyzing and comparing outcomes for various scenarios as detailed in chapter 3.

Central Indiana Reference model

The Central Indiana VisionEval strategic travel demand model is used as a critical and important tool to help identify and assess the impact of state and local actions that can affect household travel behavior and reduce regional GHG emissions. The tool is designed to run several unique scenarios to analyze various possible futures but first requires building a base or reference case model.

Establishing a reference model is key to understanding the impacts of varied policies and actions that can affect travel outcomes. The Central Indiana VisionEval reference model uses the MCCOG and Indianapolis Metropolitan Planning Organization (IMPO) travel models to represent both current and forecasted travel conditions and demographics for 2019 and 2050. The following section describes the model geographies, the inputs used and how they are derived, and the general setup used for the reference model.

Geography

{width="2.001388888888889in" height="3.8743055555555554in"}VisionEval is divided into the following geographies, each dictating the geographic resolution of various inputs and outputs:

  • Region: The entire area covered by the VisionEval model.

  • Marea: The metropolitan areas within the VisionEval model. For the Central Indiana model, the Mareas represent MPA boundaries, with all other land area classified as not being in a metropolitan area.

  • Azone: Larger geographic areas such as cities or counties. The Central Indiana model divides the metropolitan and county areas into multiple Azones.

  • Bzone: Subdivisions of Azones that are used to represent individual neighborhoods or sub-county areas such as Census Block Groups. The Central Indiana Bzones are adapted from Census Block Groups.

Inputs

VisionEval uses a series of input files to inform the model for each of the analysis years. There are 65 input files including nearly three hundred input parameters. Generally, the inputs can be classified into these general categories:

  • Setup files: Required as basic parameters for VisionEval. These values do not change.

  • Demographics inputs: Population and employee demographics and composition for the model region.

  • Land use inputs: Housing and developable land inputs to inform potential transit demand growth.

  • Systems operations/ITS inputs: Inputs that define the level of system efficiencies and ITS technology deployment.

  • Transportation options inputs: Policy inputs that impact transportation options available to households.

  • Vehicles and fuels inputs: Inputs that alter the vehicle fleet and fuel mix available to or used by households.

  • Pricing inputs: Inputs that create additional costs for system users such as congestion charges or parking costs.

Model Setup

The reference model reflects the conditions of the most current MCCOG and IMPO travel models and is calibrated to a base year of 2019 and future year of 2050. This includes demographic forecasts, land use, and transportation supply inputs including transit and roadway networks. The following summarizes the VisionEval (VE) setup and model input files for the reference scenario.

Model Run Script

The reference model uses the following VisionEval packages:

  • Population Sim: VisionEval uses the population sim module of VisionEval to create a synthetic population for the model region (VEPopulationSim).

  • Powertrains: The powertrains package adjusts the proportion of household vehicles sales by powertrain (internal combustion, hybrid, battery electric, and plug-in hybrids) for a model year. This is custom to the Central Indiana region (VEPowertrainsAndFuelsIndy).

  • Travel Demand: Working from home which includes the Multimodal Module estimated with 2017 NHTS data (VETravelDemandWFH).

  • Driverless packages: Packages which reflect updates to the driverless and car service modules. Level 3 and Level 5 automation can be modeled. Shared (e.g., Uber Pool) and unshared (typical single rider) ride hailing (car service) with different occupancies and price points (VELandUseDL and VETravelPerformanceDL). VETravelPerformanceDL includes the safety module accounting for changes in serious and fatal crashes.

Population Sim

VisionEval and PopulationSim are complementary tools that address critical aspects of transportation modeling and population synthesis. PopulationSim is an open-source platform for population synthesis, generating realistic synthetic populations from census data and user-defined control variables that is designed to support transportation planning needs.^1 VisionEval has been revised to include a module designed to incorporate PopulationSim output format as its source of synthetic population input with simple configurations that allow for different household and person-level attributes to be carried through the model.^2

Setup and Data Requirements {#setup-and-data-requirements .unnumbered}

Population Sim generates datasets of households and individuals for travel demand models using a seed sample from Census Public Use Micro Data Sample (PUMS) and control totals to align demographic and attributes with regional targets. Control totals are flexible and can be applied at various geographic levels. The control distributions can be derived from the base year Census data distributions or can be derived from a future forecasted population.

Setting up Population Sim involves several steps to define the population framework and data inputs:

  • Define total households at smallest geography: Determine the total number of households at the smallest geographic unit, such as Traffic Analysis Zones (TAZs), block groups or in this case Bzones for VE.

  • Select marginal control variables: Choose parameters Population Sim will match, typically including household size, number of workers in the household, household income ranges, and age distribution bins. Additional variables, such as race, occupation, language proficiency, or presence of children, can be included depending on needs.

  • Build marginal control distributions: For the base year, derive distributions directly from Census tables. Apply these distributions to household or person control totals. For future years, either scale the base year distributions or use forecasted data to ensure consistency with future population assumptions.

  • Assign geographic levels: Control total households at the smallest geography (e.g., TAZs or block groups). Set other distributions to be controlled at higher geographic levels, such as block groups or tracts.

The following controls are used in the PopulationSim model.

  • Household Controls

    • Size

    • Income

    • # Workers

    • Presence of Children

    • Unit type

  • Population Controls

    • Gender

    • Age

    • Group Quarter Type

    • Occupation Class

The seed data is taken from the US Census ACS PUMS (Public Use Microdata Sample) that is expanded using the above mentioned controls. The controls, taken from Census data account for the variation and distribution of the variables across the relevant geography (some controls are at county level, some down to the block group level).

There are several advantages to implementing PopulationSim. VisionEval includes a module to directly use PopulationSim outputs as its synthetic population input. PopulationSim is widely used and trusted in many travel modeling applications, making it a reliable choice. When using Census PUMS as the seed sample, any census variable can be included in the synthetic population, even if it is not part of the marginal controls. PopulationSim can match a wide range of population distributions, accommodating diverse modeling needs. PopulationSim produces unweighted outputs, ensuring that total households and persons are always represented as integer values, making the results straightforward to interpret and apply.

PopulationSim also includes a feature called 'Repop' which allows for adjustments to specific zones only in the synthetic population. This feature is for things like addition of housing in a specific neighborhood. The population can be adjusted only for the new households and the remaining population remains consistent -- reducing variability from model randomness.

The VEPowertrainsAndFuels package can be customized to match the vehicle sales by powertrain type, fuel type, and vehicle year for the model region. Multiscenario Analysis

Background

The multiscenario analysis is designed to run hundreds of VisionEval models for the Central Indiana region that combine unique changes to specific input values across several input types. This is referred to in VisionEval as the multiscenario design. In this set up, users are able to substitute base-level inputs (i.e. reference case) for alternative values that may reflect a potential alternative future. This allows users to create model runs with more or less aggressive input values based on potential policy or pricing futures and compare their effects to the base scenario. For example, users could test how DVMT may be affected by higher investment in policies that improve active travel and transit mode shares in the model region or within a Marea. Decisions on which inputs to vary and how aggressively to test them should be informed by stakeholder input, potential policy goals, or existing plans.

Input Design for the Multiscenario Analysis

These scenarios were designed with feedback from MCCOG staff. This feedback informed which inputs are varied and the relative scale of the variation. Several inputs were explored in the scenario analysis and are organized into categories, with multiple levels of change within each category. Each level represents a new degree of change to the reference model inputs. The various levels from each category are then combined to create the scenarios using unique combinations of these model inputs. The input variations resulted in 648 scenarios, not including the base model.

ITHIM

Background

The Integrated Transport and Health Impact Modeling Tool (ITHIM) was originally developed as a spreadsheet model in 2009 (Woodcock et al.) Subsequent versions of the model have been implemented in Analytica (ITHIM 2.0) and R (ITHIM-R). A handful of ITHIM models have been developed for cities across the United States, including San Francisco, CA,[^7] Nashville, TN,[^8] and Sacramento, CA.[^9] Newer US-based implementations of ITHIM have used the R version of the model (ITHIM-R).

ITHIM Data Requirements

ITHIM is a data-intensive model. Input data requirements include a synthetic population with age and sex characteristics, daily minutes spent walking and biking for transportation for members of the synthetic population, distance travelled by members of the synthetic population by travel mode, non-transportation physical activity, and other inputs. Broadly, input data requirements can be grouped into five categories:

  • Population data. Data describing the synthetic population for which health outcomes are modeled.

  • Trip data. Data describing travel behavior of the population (i.e., trip distances and durations by mode).

  • Baseline health status data. Data characterizing the baseline health status of the population.

  • Baseline exposure data. Data capturing exposures and/or behaviors that impact human health, both in the baseline and in any modelled scenarios.

  • Epidemiological/health risk data. Parameters that establish a relationship between an exposure and one or more health outcomes.

A prototype set of scripts to run ITHIM operations using outputs from VisionEval was created by FHWA as part of a recent Pooled Fund project that supported the development and enhancement of VisionEval. The prototype, referred to as VE-ITHIM, is a set of scripts that use VE output along with other pre-processed health and risk data to produce ITHIM performance measures.

ITHIM estimates yearly avoided mortality for several health outcomes: all-cause non-accidental mortality (i.e., all-cause mortality excluding accidents like traffic deaths), COPD (Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease) mortality, diabetes mortality, colon cancer mortality, and traffic deaths. The same outcome measures are also expressed as yearly avoided years of life lost (YLL), which represent the future years of life lost due to excess mortality (i.e., a mortality event for a younger individual will have a higher YLL value than the same mortality event for an older individual, all else constant).

Model inputs to the ITHIM include:

  • Particulates: bzone_pm.csv. This file contains estimates of background PM~2.5~ concentrations for each bzone in the model region: Source: Atmospheric Composition Analysis Group at Washington University in St. Louis estimates spatially joined to model Bzones.^10

  • Physical Activity: bzone_pa.csv. This file contains estimates of leisure time physical activity (LTPA) for individuals living in each bzone in the model region, presented as distribution across four physical activity levels (no activity; 40 to 74 minutes per week, 75 to 149 minutes per week, or 150 to 225 minutes per week). Source: the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) PLACES dataset^11 and Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS) survey^12, processed and allocated to model Bzones.

  • Crash Risk: marea_safety_factors.csv. This file is a copy of the marea_safety_factors.csv file that may be present on the VE model's input data.

  • Age-and sex-specific death and years of life lost: gbd.csv. The estimates for each disease present in disease_outcomes_lookup.csv (i.e., each modeled health outcome). Any health outcome estimated by the model needs to be present in this file. Source: Institute of Health Metrics and Evaluation (IHME) global burden of disease estimates.

  • Disease Crosswalk: disease_outcomes_lookup.csv: This file contains a crosswalk of all modeled health outcomes, mapping the outcome to the disease name in gbd.csv and well as in the relative risk input csvs. Any health outcome estimated by the model needs to be present in this file.

Other inputs that have used the default ITHIM values that define inhalation rates:

  • BodyMass.csv: this file contains parameters used to estimate body mass which, along with the next four input files, are required for ventilation rate calculations. Default values have been adopted from the existing ITHIM-R source code.

  • ECF.csv: this file contains parameters used to estimate individual's energy conversion factors.

  • RMR.csv: this file contains parameters used to estimate resting metabolic rates.

  • NVO2max.csv: this file contains parameters used to estimate maximum oxygen uptake rates.

  • VentilationFromOxygenUptake.csv: this file contains parameters used to estimate ventilation from oxygen uptake.

Lastly, a set of inputs in the /drpa and /drap directories contains many files that define relative risks values for various disease outcomes for the air pollution and physical activity health outcome pathways.

  • DRPA (Dose-Response for Physical Activity) is an R package developed by the ITHIM (Integrated Transport and Health Impact Modeling) team to provide dose-response functions for physical activity and various causes and diseases. It serves as a data source for the ITHIM model, supplying the Physical Activity Dose-Response Functions (DRFs) necessary to estimate health impacts.

  • DRAP. These give the exposure-response relationships between air pollution (PM2.5) and its impact on health for different health outcomes. These are accounted for in Air Pollution Exposure Response Functions (ERFs) used in ITHIM.
